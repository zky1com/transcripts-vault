# Naval - Believe in science is an oxymoron — science is a method of truth-seeking that must be questioned not a body of authority accepted on faith

## Position

The phrase "believe in science" — which became common as a political and cultural signifier — is philosophically incoherent. Science is not a body of fixed doctrine to be accepted or rejected. It is a method: conjecture, test, refute, improve. To "believe in" science in the way one believes in a religion is to misunderstand what science is at a fundamental level. Science works precisely because it does not require belief — it requires evidence, testing, and the willingness to be proven wrong. When people say "believe in science," they typically mean "defer to scientific institutions" — which is a very different claim, and one that is itself antithetical to the scientific method. Scientific institutions, like all institutions, can be wrong, corrupted, or captured. Questioning them is not anti-scientific — it is scientific. Treating their conclusions as sacred is anti-scientific.

## Logic Chain

1. Science = method (conjecture + test + refutation) — not a body of fixed doctrine
2. "Believing in" something = accepting it as true on faith without requiring evidence
3. Science requires the opposite: evidence, testing, refutation — not faith
4. "Believe in science" = accept scientific institutions' conclusions without questioning — this is faith, not science
5. Scientific institutions can be wrong (they have been, repeatedly) → treating them as infallible is anti-scientific
6. Questioning institutions is not anti-scientific — it is scientific; the willingness to be wrong is the core of the method
