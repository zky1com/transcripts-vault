# Naval - The social sciences are doubly corrupted — they depend on society for funding and their outputs drive policy making them politically motivated from both ends

## Position

The social sciences face a dual corruption that natural sciences largely avoid. On the input side, they depend on social institutions and political bodies for funding. Funding bodies have political preferences, and research that challenges those preferences is less likely to be funded. On the output side, social science results are used to drive public policy. This means researchers know their findings will have political consequences — which creates incentives to produce findings that are politically aligned with those who will implement them. Natural sciences (physics, chemistry, biology) are less vulnerable because their outputs (physical laws, molecular structures) are less directly actionable politically. Social sciences produce outputs that directly affect social and policy decisions, creating feedback loops between political incentives and research conclusions. The result is that social sciences are not merely less rigorous — they are structurally motivated to produce politically convenient results.

## Logic Chain

1. Input corruption: social science funding comes from political institutions → research that challenges political consensus gets defunded
2. Output corruption: social science findings drive policy → researchers know their outputs will affect policy → incentive to produce policy-friendly findings
3. Natural sciences: outputs (physical laws) are politically neutral → less susceptible to these feedback loops
4. Social sciences face both corruptions simultaneously → systematically biased toward politically convenient conclusions
5. Replication crisis and other quality problems in social sciences are downstream of these structural incentive problems
