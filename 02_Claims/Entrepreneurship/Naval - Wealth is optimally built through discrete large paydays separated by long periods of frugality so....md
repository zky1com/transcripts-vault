# Naval - Wealth is optimally built through discrete large paydays separated by long periods of frugality so...

## Position

The tech industry's wealth-creation pattern â€” years of low/no income followed by a single large liquidity event â€” is structurally superior for accumulating real capital compared to incremental salary growth. The gap between paydays is long enough that lifestyle cannot adapt to the new income level before the next frugal period begins. This "lump payday" structure preserves the entire gain as capital rather than losing it to lifestyle creep. High marginal tax rates on peak-year income are therefore especially damaging to this model, because they treat a decade of losses + one big year as simply "high income" and tax accordingly.

## Evidence Cited

- "you want to make your money in discrete lumps separated over long periods of time so that your own lifestyle does not have a chance to adapt quickly"
- Tech industry pattern: "you don't make any money for ten years and then suddenly in year 11 you might have a giant payday"
- Critique of high marginal tax rates: "the highest risk-taking most creative professions... bleed and bleed and bleed and then suddenly have one single big payday" and then get taxed as if they were simply rich that year

## Logic Chain

1. Lifestyle adaptation is not instantaneous â€” it takes months to years to upgrade to a new income level
2. If income arrives in one large lump after a long frugal period, lifestyle has not pre-adapted to that level
3. The entire lump is therefore available as capital before lifestyle consumes it
4. This is why tech founders who live frugally through a decade of poverty and then exit can achieve durable wealth
5. Progressive tax systems designed around continuous high earners are structurally mismatched with this lump-payday model
