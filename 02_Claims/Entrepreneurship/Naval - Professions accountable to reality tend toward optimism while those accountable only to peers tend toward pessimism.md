# Naval - Professions accountable to reality tend toward optimism while those accountable only to peers tend toward pessimism

## Position

There is a systematic relationship between where a profession gets its feedback and what worldview it tends to produce. Professions that get feedback from physical reality (engineering, farming, medicine with patients) or from free markets (entrepreneurs, traders) tend to produce optimists. They cannot afford sustained pessimism because their work demands that problems be solvable and that solutions exist. Professions that get feedback primarily from professional peers — academic journalists impressing other journalists, restaurateurs aiming for foodie awards, ivory-tower academics — tend to produce pessimists. Their status depends on performing sophisticated concern rather than solving problems. The optimist-entrepreneur nexus is structural: you cannot start a company without believing that value can be created that doesn't yet exist. Pessimism about a domain effectively disqualifies you from building in it.

## Evidence Cited

- "the people who are operating in the real world and are getting paid for it tend to be optimists the people who are operating ivory towers are incented to be pessimists"
- "to be an entrepreneur you need to be optimistic about the fact that you're creating something that other people are going to find value in"
- "when you see a journalist writing articles to impress other journalists...those end up not being practical and high quality"

## Logic Chain

1. Feedback source determines which behaviors are selected for in a profession
2. Reality/market feedback: solutions either work or they don't → selection pressure for realistic optimism (problems are solvable)
3. Peer feedback: status depends on sophisticated concern and critical positioning → selection for pessimism as a signal of intelligence
4. Entrepreneurs cannot start companies while believing value cannot be created → structural selection for optimism
5. Therefore: the distribution of optimism vs pessimism across professions is not random — it tracks the nature of the feedback mechanism
