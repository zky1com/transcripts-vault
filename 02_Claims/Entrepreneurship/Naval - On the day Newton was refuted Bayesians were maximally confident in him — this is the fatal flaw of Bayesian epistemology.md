# Naval - On the day Newton was refuted Bayesians were maximally confident in him — this is the fatal flaw of Bayesian epistemology

## Position

Bayesian epistemology says: accumulate evidence, update your credence in a theory upward with each confirming experiment. Prior to 1919, every experiment done on Newton's theory of gravity confirmed it. A Bayesian should have been maximally confident in Newton on the eve of Eddington's eclipse experiment — that was when the most evidence had accumulated. But that was also the day before Newton was refuted. This reveals a structural flaw in Bayesian epistemology: it makes you most confident in a theory at the worst possible moment. The Popperian alternative does not have this problem: Newton's theory was never "true" — it always contained error, the error just hadn't been detected yet. The theory was never a legitimate object of accumulating confidence; it was always a provisional best-guess subject to future refutation.

## Evidence Cited

- "prior to 1919 approximately every single experiment that was done on Newton's theory of gravity showed that it was consistent with Newton's theory"
- "what does a Bayesian say in that situation? what a Bayesian has to say is: getting more and more confident in Newton's theory"
- "the day before it was shown to be false was the day when you were most confident in it — how do you square that circle?"
- "the Popperian doesn't have this problem — Popper just says at no point was Newton's theory actually true — it contains some truth but that truth isn't a thing that we can measure"

## Logic Chain

1. Bayesian epistemology: P(theory | evidence) increases with each confirming experiment
2. Newton's theory: confirmed by every experiment from ~1687 to 1919
3. Bayesian conclusion pre-1919: confidence in Newton's theory was at maximum (most evidence accumulated)
4. 1919: Eddington's eclipse experiment; Newton's theory refuted; Einstein's GR confirmed as better
5. Bayesian paradox: maximum confidence was reached precisely when the theory was about to be refuted
6. Popperian resolution: confidence is never the right attitude toward a theory; Newton's theory was always provisional/false — it just hadn't been refuted yet
7. Accumulating confirmations does not increase the probability that a theory is correct — it only confirms that it hasn't been refuted yet in those specific conditions
