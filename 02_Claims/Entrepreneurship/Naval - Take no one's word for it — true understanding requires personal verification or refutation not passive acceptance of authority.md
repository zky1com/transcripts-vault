# Naval - Take no one's word for it — true understanding requires personal verification or refutation not passive acceptance of authority

## Position

The Royal Society motto "nullius in verba" — take no one's word for it — states the epistemological standard for genuine knowledge. You do not truly know something because an authority told you it was true, even a highly credible authority. You know it when you have verified it yourself or have seriously attempted to refute it and failed. Naval's approach to Deutsch's Beginning of Infinity exemplifies this: he read it to confirm or refute the principles for himself, not simply to absorb what Deutsch said. This is demanding but necessary. Authority-based belief is fragile — the moment the authority is shown to be wrong in one domain, the belief structure built on that authority becomes suspect. Personally-verified knowledge is more robust: you understand the evidence and the argument, so you can maintain the belief correctly even if the source authority turns out to be flawed in other areas.

## Logic Chain

1. Authority-based belief: X says P → I believe P
2. Flaw: if X is wrong about Q, my belief in P (which also rests only on X's authority) becomes uncertain
3. Personal verification: I examine the evidence and argument for P independently → I believe P because of the evidence, not because of X
4. Personally-verified belief survives the undermining of the original authority
5. "Nullius in verba" operationalizes this: treat every claim as a conjecture to be verified or refuted, regardless of source
6. The process of attempting refutation (and failing) is the mechanism that converts a claim into knowledge
