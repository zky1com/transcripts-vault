# Matt Ridley - Innovation is evolutionary not heroic — lone inventors never existed and ideas are a dime a dozen

## Position

The popular history of innovation centers on the lone genius: Wright Brothers, Thomas Edison, Alexander Graham Bell, Newton. This is systematically misleading. In virtually every case, the credited inventor was working in a network of collaborators, building on prior work, and racing against contemporaries who were close to the same discovery. Edison described it as 1% inspiration and 99% perspiration — and most of the perspiration was collaborative. Innovation is an evolutionary process: a population of tinkerers generates many variations, the environment (market, customers, physical laws) selects the ones that work, and the result gets attributed to whoever made the final incremental step. Ideas are a dime a dozen; innovation is the long, expensive, collaborative process of turning an idea into something practical, affordable, and reliable that people actually want to use.

## Evidence Cited

- "I try and draw a distinction between invention and innovation — invention is coming up with a prototype of a new device — innovation is the business of turning a new device into something practical, affordable, and reliable that people want to use"
- "it was Edison who said this is a process of 1% inspiration and 99% perspiration — what I'm trying to do in this book is rescue the perspiration from obscurity"
- "the beaver saying to the rabbit: 'no I didn't build it, but it's based on an idea of mine' — that is how inventors quite often think about innovation"
- "these things do work — they just need that right structure of previous innovations to build on top of"

## Logic Chain

1. Invention = prototype of new device (one person, one moment)
2. Innovation = turning invention into practical, affordable, reliable product that people adopt
3. Innovation requires: driving down costs, driving up reliability, persuading adoption — all long collaborative processes
4. The "great inventor" typically placed the last stone in an arch others built
5. Nobel prizes and patents tend to identify one name from a team of collaborators → creates misleading great-man narrative
6. Ideas are ubiquitous — the dot-com bubble showed that many ideas that failed in 1999 succeeded as Instacart, DoorDash etc in 2015
7. Timing, infrastructure, customer base, and the ecosystem of prior innovations matter more than the originating idea
