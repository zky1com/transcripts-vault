## Position

A principle from complexity theory: whenever you find a complex working system in nature, it was produced by iterating a simple design, not by designing complexity up front. The reverse â€” designing a complex system from scratch and trying to make it work â€” produces systems that fall apart. The modern AI scaling results confirm this: simple attention mechanisms iterated over massive amounts of data produce emergent intelligence. Product design follows the same pattern: iterate toward the simple thing that works, then remove accumulated complexity. The Raptor engine moved from highly configurable (many adjustable parts) to hard to vary (almost nothing left to remove).

## Evidence Cited

- "There's a theory in complexity theory that whenever you find a complex system working in nature, it's usually the output of a very simple system or thing that was iterated over and over"
- "You're just taking very simple algorithms and dumping more and more data into them. They keep getting smarter. What doesn't work as well is the reverse. When you design a very complex system and then you try to make a functioning large system out of that, it just falls apart"
- "a lot of product design is iterating on your own designs until you find the simple thing that works. And often you've added stuff around it that you don't need and then you have to go back and extract the simplicity back out of the noise"

## Logic Chain

1. Nature and AI both exhibit the same pattern: simple rules iterated produce emergent complexity
2. Attempting the reverse (designing complexity top-down) produces systems too fragile to function
3. Good product design is therefore a reduction process: iterate until the simple core is found, then remove everything that isn't necessary
4. This is why evolved systems and mature products converge toward "hard to vary" â€” they've removed everything not essential
5. Therefore: the design goal is not to add the right features but to iterate toward the minimal set that achieves the goal
