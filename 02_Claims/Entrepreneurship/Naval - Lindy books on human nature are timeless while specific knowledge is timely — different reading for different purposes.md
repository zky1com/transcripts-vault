# Naval - Lindy books on human nature are timeless while specific knowledge is timely — different reading for different purposes

## Position

There is a systematic bifurcation in what is worth reading. Books about human nature — psychology, philosophy of human behavior, character, motivation — are Lindy: they survive the test of time because human nature does not change. An 1820s essay on envy or ambition is as accurate as one written today. By contrast, books about specific domains — technology, science, markets, business — decay rapidly because the underlying landscape changes. Both categories have value but require different reading strategies: go to old books for human nature; stay on the bleeding edge for applied knowledge. What does not make sense is reading old books that are neither about human nature nor Lindy — outdated specific-domain knowledge that is both stale and not about timeless human patterns.

## Evidence Cited

- "when it comes to anything about human nature, I say go read the Lindy books, the older books, the ones that have survived the test of time"
- "but if you want to develop specific knowledge, get paid for it, do something useful, then you want to stay on the bleeding edge — that knowledge is going to be more timely and obsolete more quickly"
- "what doesn't make sense to me is just reading stuff that's not Lindy or that's not about human nature but is old"

## Logic Chain

1. Human nature is constant across centuries → books about human nature do not decay
2. The Lindy effect: books that have survived 200 years have proven relevance → expected to survive another 200
3. Specific knowledge (technology, markets, science) changes rapidly → old books in these domains are mostly obsolete
4. Therefore: two valid strategies: (a) old Lindy books on human nature; (b) current bleeding-edge works on specific domains
5. Invalid strategy: old non-Lindy books on specific topics — stale without the timelessness benefit
6. Schopenhauer example: his insights on human nature still apply; his scientific views are obviously outdated → read the former, not the latter
