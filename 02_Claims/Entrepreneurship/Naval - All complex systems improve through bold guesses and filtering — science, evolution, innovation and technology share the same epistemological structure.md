# Naval - All complex systems improve through bold guesses and filtering — science, evolution, innovation and technology share the same epistemological structure

## Position

Across every domain of complex improvement, the same underlying structure repeats: generate variations, test them against reality, eliminate the failures. Evolution does it via random mutation and natural selection. Popperian science does it via bold conjectures and empirical refutation. Edison's innovation lab did it via trial and error — creative guesses, tried out, filtered by what actually works. Technology development does it via prototype-and-test cycles. This is not a coincidence. It reflects something deep about how knowledge grows in any system facing a complex, partially-understood environment: you cannot derive the right answer from first principles, so you must generate candidates and filter. The implication for individuals is that your personal epistemology — how you generate and test beliefs — should follow the same structure: make bold guesses, expose them to reality, eliminate the ones that fail.

## Evidence Cited

- "it's not just science when we look at innovation and technology and building for example everything that thomas edison did and nikola tesla did these were from trial and error which is creative guesses and trying things out"
- "if you look at how evolution works through variation and then natural selection where it tries a lot of random mutations and it filters out the ones that didn't work so this seems to be a general model through which all complex systems improve themselves over time they make bold guesses and then they weed out the things that didn't work"

## Logic Chain

1. Science: conjecture + refutation → surviving theories (Popper)
2. Evolution: random variation + natural selection → adapted organisms (Darwin)
3. Technology/innovation: trial + error → working solutions (Edison)
4. All three: generate candidates → filter by reality → retain survivors
5. This structure is universal because complex systems cannot be designed top-down from first principles — exploration + filtering is the only viable search strategy
