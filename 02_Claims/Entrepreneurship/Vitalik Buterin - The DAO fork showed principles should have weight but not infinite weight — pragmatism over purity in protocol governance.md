# Vitalik Buterin - The DAO fork showed principles should have weight but not infinite weight — pragmatism over purity in protocol governance

## Position

The DAO hack in 2016 forced Ethereum to choose between two principles: immutability (never intervene in smart contract outcomes) and protecting users from an extraordinary exploit. The decision to hard fork and reverse the hack was controversial. Vitalik's conclusion in retrospect: the fork was the right decision, but the more important lesson is that principles should have weight without having infinite weight. Purists who commit absolutely to principles eventually face a scenario extreme enough that they must compromise — and having committed maximally, their rhetorical position collapses completely. Admitting from the start that principles are not absolute maintains more integrity in the long run. The counter-precedent was set: Ethereum didn't fix the parity wallet hack a year later, which demonstrated that the DAO fork was a genuine exception rather than a template for ongoing intervention.

## Evidence Cited

- "the way I interpret the stake in the ground is that it was a moral statement that said you can believe in principles without assigning those principles infinite amount of weight"
- "most people that try to take a more purist approach end up hitting a scenario that is extreme enough that you have to compromise — and if you committed too hard to never compromising then your rhetorical posture becomes even more screwed up"
- "it's better to be transparent about these things early on"
- "people valued not fixing the parity wallet a year later in order to set the counter-precedent that we actually do take this seriously"

## Logic Chain

1. DAO hack: attacker would receive stolen ETH after time delay; smart contract immutability principle would protect the attacker
2. Purist position: never intervene — code is law — maintain immutability absolutely
3. Pragmatist position: this is an extraordinary case; one-time intervention to protect users
4. Ethereum chose pragmatism: hard fork reversed the hack
5. Risk of precedent: if we intervene once, will we always intervene?
6. Counter-precedent set: parity wallet hack (later, also large) was NOT fixed → demonstrated the DAO fork was a genuine exception
7. Vitalik's lesson: principles should have weight (not zero) but not infinite weight (not unconditional)
8. Purists who commit maximally face rhetorical collapse when they inevitably compromise under extreme circumstances
