# Naval - You cannot manufacture intrinsic motivation in people so only work with those already excited about...

## Position

You can inspire someone once â€” a rousing speech can get people to charge into battle. But you cannot sustain someone else's motivation over months and years. Long-term motivation must be intrinsic; external motivation fades and the person becomes dependent on you as a crutch. Naval's rule: he almost won't start a company or hire someone if they aren't already genuinely into what he wants them to do.

## Evidence Cited

- "I almost won't start a company or hire a person or work with somebody if I just don't think they're into what I want to do"
- "You can sell someone into doing something but you can't keep them motivated. You can get them inspired initially."
- Henry V analogy: "it might work if you're a king like Henry the Fifth trying to get them to just charge in the battle â€” but if you're trying to keep someone motivated for the long term, that motivation has to come intrinsically"
- "You can't just create it nor can you be the crutch for them if they don't have that intrinsic motivation"
- Younger self mistake: "when I was younger I used to try and talk people into things"

## Logic Chain

1. Extrinsic motivation (speeches, incentives, pressure) can produce short-term bursts
2. Long-term performance requires intrinsic motivation â€” caring about the work itself
3. You cannot sustainably manufacture caring in someone who doesn't have it
4. Trying to do so makes you a crutch and creates dependency, not reliability
5. Therefore the selection filter must happen before hiring: only engage people already excited
