# Matt Ridley - Innovation clusters geographically because innovators borrow from nearby neighbors — Silicon Valley is an ecosystem

## Position

If innovation were driven by lone brilliant individuals, it would be geographically dispersed — geniuses are born everywhere. Instead, innovation clusters intensely in specific places at specific times: Victorian Britain, Song Dynasty China, Renaissance Italy, and currently Silicon Valley. The explanation is that innovation is an evolutionary process where each tinkerer borrows a little bit from nearby neighbors. Geographic proximity allows this borrowing to happen at maximum density: ideas travel at a cocktail party, turn into a prototype in a coffee shop conversation, get funded by a local VC who heard about it through a portfolio company, and spread through a network of interconnected innovators. In Silicon Valley, this network effect is compounded by a triple coincidence: the talent base, the venture capital, and the early adopter customers (other tech companies) are all in the same place. This triple coincidence is very hard to assemble elsewhere.

## Evidence Cited

- "the key to this is understanding the ecosystem in which these innovative people operate — because they're not only getting ideas from each other and daring each other on to be innovators but they're also directly borrowing technologies"
- "in Silicon Valley we have a critical mass of thousands of innovative companies that will adopt products from each other — so you not only find your innovator base and your talent base in one place but you also find your customer base"
- "it became clear to me that it couldn't have happened without all the other industries around it producing the high-quality metals and chemicals that were necessary for this process"
- "why is the bushfire only burning in one place at one time? The key is understanding the ecosystem"

## Logic Chain

1. If innovation = lone genius: expect geographic dispersion (geniuses born everywhere)
2. Observation: innovation is intensely geographically clustered in one hot zone per era
3. Explanation: innovation is evolutionary borrowing — each tinkerer borrows from nearby tinkerers
4. Geographic proximity maximizes the rate of borrowing: ideas spread through informal contact
5. Silicon Valley compounds this: talent + VC + early adopter customers all in one place = triple network effect
6. Industrial ecosystems matter too: the Haber process required adjacent chemical and metallurgy industries
7. Early adopter customers = other tech companies → you can sell unfinished products and iterate faster
8. Result: each new company in the cluster makes every other company marginally more innovative
