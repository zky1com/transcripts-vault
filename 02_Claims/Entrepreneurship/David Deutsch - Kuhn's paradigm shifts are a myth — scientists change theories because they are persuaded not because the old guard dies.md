# David Deutsch - Kuhn's paradigm shifts are a myth — scientists change theories because they are persuaded not because the old guard dies

## Position

Kuhn's account of scientific revolutions depicts them as generational: a young iconoclast proposes a new paradigm, the old guard (incapable of conceiving of the new framework) resists, and the paradigm shift happens only when the old guard literally dies and is replaced by a generation that grew up with the new theory. Deutsch argues this is pure fiction. He cannot identify a real historical case that fits this pattern. What actually happens is that people — of any age — change theories when they are persuaded by argument. Even the famous cases used to support Kuhnian mythology (Semmelweis, Lister) don't fit: the resistance to antiseptic procedures wasn't generational but professional, driven by working-practice concerns about perceived dignity and authority. There was no age stratification. Progress happens through persuasion and argument, not through the death of dissenters.

## Evidence Cited

- "I don't know of any actual situation where that happened — what does happen is that people often irrationally stick to their own ideas whether they are new ideas or old ideas"
- "the generational story — as I say in Fabric of Reality — provides no explanation for them changing from one theory to another — it's as if they just invent a new fashion"
- "even then it was not a generational thing — there was a much more complex process at work — they didn't just reject a theory — they rejected having to change their working practices"
- "it's pure fiction"

## Logic Chain

1. Kuhn's claim: paradigm shifts require generational turnover — old guard literally can't conceive of new paradigm
2. Prediction: when revolution happens, it should correlate with the death of the old generation
3. Deutsch's empirical challenge: he cannot find historical examples where this is what actually happened
4. Semmelweis/Lister counterexample: resistance was not generational — it was about perceived professional dignity and working-practice disruption; mixed ages on both sides
5. What actually happens: people adopt new theories when persuaded — through argument, evidence, demonstration
6. This can happen to people of any age; resistance can come from people of any age
7. Kuhn's theory provides no mechanism for adoption either — "like a new fashion" — this is not an explanation, it is a metaphor
