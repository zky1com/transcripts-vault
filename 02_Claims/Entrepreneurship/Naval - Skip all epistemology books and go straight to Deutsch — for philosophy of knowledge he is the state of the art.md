# Naval - Skip all epistemology books and go straight to Deutsch — for philosophy of knowledge he is the state of the art

## Position

The conventional path to learning epistemology involves working through the history of philosophy: Plato, Aristotle, Hume, Kant, Popper, and then Deutsch. Naval's view is that this path is largely unnecessary. Deutsch represents the current state of the art — his theory of knowledge (good explanations, fallibilism, no justificationism) supersedes the earlier theories rather than merely extending them. Reading the history helps with context: it lets you understand what Deutsch is refuting and why. But if your goal is understanding epistemology rather than its history, Deutsch is where you should go. One qualification: for some readers, reading Popper, Taleb, or other adjacent thinkers first helps them appreciate what Deutsch is saying because they arrive with the prior beliefs that Deutsch's arguments are designed to refute.

## Evidence Cited

- "for the state-of-the-art on the philosophy of knowledge which people call epistemology, you can basically skip everything and jump straight to David Deutsch — if you just want to know epistemology, read David Deutsch, full stop"
- "that said, for some people it helps to know the history, the counterarguments, where he's coming from, the existing theories of knowledge like the justified true belief theory or the inductive theory of knowledge"
- "so many people believe in that that if you just read Deutsch, you would see him shooting down these things, but you yourself would not have those things in solid footing"

## Logic Chain

1. Deutsch's theory of knowledge (Beginning of Infinity) is the current best theory of epistemology
2. It supersedes prior theories (JTB theory, inductivism, Bayesianism) rather than merely extending them
3. Reading the history helps those who arrive with prior beliefs that Deutsch refutes → gives them the "solid footing" they need
4. But the history is not necessary for understanding Deutsch's position — it is context, not prerequisite
5. Efficient path: read Deutsch → if unclear, read some context (Popper, Taleb) → return to Deutsch with better footing
6. Inefficient path: read full history → arrive at Deutsch already exhausted by less correct theories
