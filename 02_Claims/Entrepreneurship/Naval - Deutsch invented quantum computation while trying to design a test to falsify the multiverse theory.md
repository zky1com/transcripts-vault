# Naval - Deutsch invented quantum computation while trying to design a test to falsify the multiverse theory

## Position

The origin of quantum computation is not primarily a computer science or engineering story — it is an epistemological story. Deutsch held the many-worlds (multiverse) interpretation of quantum mechanics and was trying to design an experiment that would falsify it. To design such an experiment, he needed to imagine what a quantum AI observing a superposition would experience — whether the AI's observation would collapse the wave function. To reason about a quantum AI, he needed to understand what quantum computation would look like. There was no theory of quantum computation at the time. So Deutsch invented it. The practical field of quantum computing is therefore a byproduct of an attempt to falsify a physics interpretation, itself driven by the Popperian requirement that every good theory must in principle be refutable. This is a striking example of knowledge creating knowledge in unexpected directions.

## Evidence Cited

- "he actually came up with the theory of quantum computation and extended the Church-Turing conjecture into the Church-Turing-Deutsch conjecture when he was trying to come up with a way to falsify his theory of the multiverse"
- "to invent the experiment for how to falsify the multiverse theory, he had to in his mind imagine an AGI and get inside the AGI's brain and say if that AGI is observing something, does it collapse?"
- "how do you even create a quantum AGI? We don't have quantum computers. Okay, we need quantum computers. So he came up with the theory of quantum computation and that launched the field of quantum computing"

## Logic Chain

1. Deutsch holds many-worlds interpretation of QM (multiverse)
2. Good theory must be falsifiable (Popper/Deutsch epistemology)
3. To falsify multiverse: need experiment distinguishing it from alternatives
4. Thought experiment: quantum AI observing a superposition — does it collapse? → depends on whether AI is "inside" the wave function
5. To reason rigorously about this: need a formal theory of quantum computation
6. No such theory existed → Deutsch invented it
7. Side effect: launched the entire field of quantum computing
8. This is knowledge-creation spawning more knowledge-creation in unexpected directions
