# Naval - The best authors respect the reader's time — high density of ideas per paragraph is the mark of great writing

## Position

The primary quality signal for a written work is the density of ideas per unit of reading time. An author who packs genuine insights into every paragraph — where a single sentence can send a reader into an hour of reflection — is respecting the reader's time. An author who writes at high volume with low density is wasting it. Naval uses Schopenhauer as the archetype: his shorter essays have the density of tweets — one paragraph, one thinking session. This is also why he values Deutsch and certain fiction writers. The inverse (high volume, low density) is exemplified by the history books he avoids — vast narrative works where the ideas-per-page ratio is too low. This is not an anti-long-form argument; it is a pro-density argument. A dense 12-volume work would be fine; a sprawling 12-volume work where a single dense summary captures the essential ideas is worse than the summary.

## Evidence Cited

- "the best authors respect the reader's time and Schopenhauer is very much in that vein"
- "he has high density of ideas, very well thought through, good minimal examples and analogies — you can pick it up and read one paragraph and you're thinking for the next hour"
- "we do want to read high-density work"
- "Deutsch is extremely high density. Borges is very high density. Ted Chang is very high density"

## Logic Chain

1. Reader has finite time and finite attention
2. High-density writing: each paragraph contains a novel idea + minimal examples + clear reasoning → maximizes value per time unit
3. Low-density writing: same ideas stretched across more pages → wastes reader's time
4. Best authors internalize the reader's time constraint as a design principle
5. Result: Schopenhauer's essays > his longer philosophical works; dense summaries > exhaustive volumes; Twitter-like aphorisms at their best
6. This is compatible with long works — the criterion is density, not brevity
