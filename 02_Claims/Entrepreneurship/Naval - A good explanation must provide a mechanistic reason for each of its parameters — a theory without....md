# Naval - A good explanation must provide a mechanistic reason for each of its parameters — a theory without...

## Position

The grass-cure-for-the-common-cold example illustrates a deeper failure than just easy variability: the theory provides no mechanism by which grass could cure a cold. Without a mechanism, there is no basis for choosing among the infinite possible parameter values (1.0 kg vs 1.1 kg vs a different species of grass vs a different day). The mechanism is what makes the parameters *determinate* â€” what tells you why this number rather than another. A good explanation must provide the reason each component is what it is. Testability without mechanism is arbitrary â€” you can test it, but you have no reason to believe the test result tells you anything general.

## Evidence Cited

- "the problem is that no one should test it why because they haven't given you an explanation as to what the mechanism is that would enable grass to cure the common cold"
- "it has to be very precise and there must be a good reason for the precision"

## Logic Chain

1. The grass theory specifies 1.0 kilograms â€” but gives no reason for that specific quantity
2. Without a mechanism explaining why grass cures colds, there is no basis for choosing 1.0 over 1.1 or 0.5
3. This means the parameter is arbitrary â€” and an arbitrary parameter can always be adjusted to absorb a failed test
4. A mechanism is what makes parameters non-arbitrary: "the Earth is tilted at 23 degrees because of angular momentum conservation" explains *why* 23 degrees
5. Therefore: a mechanistic basis is a prerequisite for a good explanation, not just a nice-to-have
