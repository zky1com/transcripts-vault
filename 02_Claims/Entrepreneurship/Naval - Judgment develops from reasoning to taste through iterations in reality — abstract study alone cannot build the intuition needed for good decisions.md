# Naval - Judgment develops from reasoning to taste through iterations in reality — abstract study alone cannot build the intuition needed for good decisions

## Position

Naval describes a three-stage development of decision-making ability. The first stage is reasoning: applying explicit logical chains to problems, step by step. This is slow, error-prone, and requires conscious effort. Through many iterations in real situations, reasoning accumulates into judgment: a refined capacity to assess situations quickly with a higher hit rate, drawing on pattern recognition built through experience. At its most developed, judgment becomes taste or intuition — the ability to see immediately what the right action is without consciously running through a chain of reasoning. This final stage cannot be built from abstract study. It requires actual iterations in reality because it is built from compressed experience, not from propositions. A person who has only studied principles operates at the reasoning stage at best; they cannot develop taste without doing.

## Evidence Cited

- "you start with reasoning and then you build up your judgment and then when your judgment is sufficiently refined, it just becomes taste or intuition or gut feel and that's what you operate on"
- "Acquiring knowledge is easy. The hard part is knowing what to apply and when. That's why all true learning is on the job."

## Logic Chain

1. Stage 1 — reasoning: explicit logical chains, slow, deliberate, error-prone in novel situations
2. Stage 2 — judgment: accumulated from many iterations in reality, faster and more accurate
3. Stage 3 — taste/intuition: compressed experience; correct action appears directly without conscious reasoning
4. Each stage requires the previous one plus real iterations — you cannot skip from reasoning to taste via study
5. Implication: abstract study can accelerate development but cannot substitute for the iterative experience that builds judgment into taste
