# Naval - Having two competing viable scientific theories is rare and three is almost unknown — creativity no...

## Position

Scientific progress is not normally a competition between multiple viable theories. In practice, there is almost always just one accepted theory in any given domain. Having two simultaneously viable theories is exceptional â€” Newton vs. Einstein on gravity is one of the rare cases. Having three competing viable theories is almost unheard of in the history of science. This matters because Bayesian updating â€” the idea that you revise probability weights across competing hypotheses as new data arrives â€” only applies once you already have the competing hypotheses. You cannot Bayesian-update your way to a new theory that doesn't yet exist. The generation of new theories is an act of creativity, not calculation. Bayes tells you which of your existing theories is better supported. It cannot tell you what theories you haven't thought of yet. The set of "all possible theories" is not enumerable in advance â€” which is exactly where creativity enters. Induction and Bayesianism work well in finite known spaces; they fail completely at the problem of generating new explanations.

## Logic Chain

1. Normal state of science: one dominant theory per domain (not multiple competitors)
2. Two viable competing theories: exceptional (Newton vs. Einstein on gravity)
3. Three viable competing theories: almost unknown in all of scientific history
4. Bayesian updating requires a prior set of theories to assign probabilities to â€” you cannot update on theories that don't yet exist
5. The creation of a new theory is an act of creativity that cannot be reduced to probability calculation
6. Bayesianism is valid and powerful for its actual domain: updating within a finite known hypothesis space
7. Bayesianism fails completely at the problem it is often claimed to solve: generating new explanations
