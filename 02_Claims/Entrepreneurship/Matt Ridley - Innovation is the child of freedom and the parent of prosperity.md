# Matt Ridley - Innovation is the child of freedom and the parent of prosperity

## Position

This two-directional causal claim is the thesis of How Innovation Works. Innovation is the child of freedom: it requires the freedom to experiment, fail, try new approaches, borrow from others, and bring products to market without requiring prior permission. Everywhere innovation has flourished, unusual degrees of freedom for entrepreneurs have been present. Innovation is the parent of prosperity: all the long-run growth in human living standards — longer lives, less famine, more abundance — can be traced to the cumulative compounding of innovations. Restricting innovation to protect existing players or avoid risk does not just slow growth; it freezes prosperity at its current level. Freedom is not just morally preferable but economically necessary as the precondition for the compounding improvements that produce human flourishing.

## Evidence Cited

- "innovation is the child of freedom and the parent of prosperity"
- "innovation is a process of evolution — like any process of evolution it requires trial and error — innovation happens by taking the body of innovators that surround you one step further, engaging in lots of trial and having error and feedback from customers"
- "you need to have the ability to take lots of tries — you need venture capital, you need startups, you need a friendly environment to start a business — and then error: we don't like people making error anymore, so we try to cover the downside risk but by doing that we also cut off the upside"

## Logic Chain

1. Innovation requires: freedom to try, fail, iterate, borrow, and reach customers
2. Everywhere innovation flourished: historically unusual levels of entrepreneurial freedom
3. Everywhere freedom was restricted: innovation clusters collapsed (see chiefs/priests/thieves)
4. Therefore: freedom is the necessary condition for innovation (child of freedom)
5. All long-run prosperity growth traces to cumulative innovation: food, medicine, energy, transport
6. Therefore: restricting innovation freezes prosperity at current levels
7. Therefore: innovation is the cause of prosperity (parent of prosperity)
8. Both directions of the causal claim have strong historical support
