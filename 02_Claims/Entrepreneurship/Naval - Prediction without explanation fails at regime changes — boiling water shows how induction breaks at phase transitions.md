# Naval - Prediction without explanation fails at regime changes — boiling water shows how induction breaks at phase transitions

## Position

A purely predictive approach to science — extrapolating observed trends without understanding the underlying mechanism — produces confident but wrong predictions whenever the system changes regime. The boiling water example is definitive: if you record water temperature rising at 10°C per minute and extrapolate, you predict it will reach 1000°C after two hours. This is wrong. At 100°C, the temperature plateaus as the phase transition consumes energy (latent heat). No amount of past trend data could predict this plateau — only an explanatory model of particle kinetics and phase transitions can. Inductive trend-fitting cannot detect the structural change that produces the plateau because the plateau requires understanding a mechanism that is invisible in the raw temperature numbers. This is the general problem: predictions without explanations work inside regimes and fail catastrophically at regime boundaries.

## Evidence Cited

- "if you're a thoroughgoing inductivist or even a Bayesian reasoner and you don't know anything about the boiling temperature, you can join all of those lovely lines into a perfectly diagonal straight line and extrapolate off into infinity"
- "after two hours according to your Bayesian reasoning, according to your induction, we should assume that the temperature of that water will be a thousand degrees celsius — but of course this is completely false"
- "there's no possible way of knowing this without first doing the experiment or having already guessed via some explanatory means what was going to happen — no method of recording all of these data points and extrapolating off into the future could ever have given you the correct answer"
- "the correct answer can only come from creativity"

## Logic Chain

1. Observation: water temperature rises linearly at constant rate
2. Inductive prediction: extrapolate trend → temperature = 1000°C after 2 hours
3. Reality: at 100°C, phase transition occurs; latent heat absorbs energy → temperature plateaus
4. The plateau is invisible in temperature data alone — requires mechanical explanation (particle kinetics, latent heat)
5. No inductive method over temperature data can predict the plateau; the mechanism is in a different level of description
6. Only an explanatory model (particle kinetics → escape velocity → phase transition) can predict when and why the plateau occurs
7. General principle: induction works within a regime; explanation is needed to predict regime changes
