# Naval - Longevity is desirable but changes the psychological problem set rather than solving it — the existential challenge persists at longer timescales

## Position

Naval's position is pro-longevity, but carefully qualified. Extending life does not dissolve the existential problem — it displaces it. The problem changes from "how do I avoid dying before I see my children grow up" to "how do I avoid boredom across centuries" to, at the limit, "how do I avoid loneliness as contemporaries die while I continue." The goods of longevity are real — more time for projects, relationships, and growth. But the challenges are real too, and they are not trivially solvable. Most of human psychology and philosophy was built for normal lifespans, and it is not obvious that the tools we have are adequate for very long ones. The right conclusion is not to oppose longevity but to take these challenges seriously rather than assuming more time is simply better. Naval frames this as a genuine open problem.

## Logic Chain

1. Longevity eliminates the early death problem — more time is better than less, all else equal
2. But longevity creates new problems: boredom (games are exhausted), loneliness (contemporaries die), loss of reference points
3. These problems are not solved by the same tools as the original problem (health, medicine)
4. Human psychology and philosophy are calibrated for ~80-year lifespans — not centuries
5. Therefore: longevity is good but not a silver bullet — it replaces one set of existential challenges with another
