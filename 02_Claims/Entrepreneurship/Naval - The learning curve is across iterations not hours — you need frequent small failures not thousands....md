# Naval - The learning curve is across iterations not hours — you need frequent small failures not thousands...

## Position

The common wisdom is "10,000 hours" â€” but Naval argues the learning curve is actually across 10,000 *iterations*, not 10,000 hours. A retail store owner who opens every day and stocks the same shelves in the same way for 10 years puts in thousands of hours but learns little. The same person constantly experimenting â€” new marketing experiments, changing inventory, changing messaging, testing different hours, talking to other store owners â€” puts in fewer hours but far more iterations, and learns exponentially more. The implication: you must get very comfortable with frequent small failures. Nassim Taleb made his fortune by losing small amounts every day and then winning large when the unthinkable happened. Entrepreneurs bleed every day (no salary, constant stress) but win large later. The crowd wants to make small profits every day with large blow-up risk; the right approach inverts this.

## Logic Chain

1. 10,000 hours of the same task = 10,000 hours of repetition, not learning
2. Learning happens at the edge â€” first attempts at new things, not nth repetitions of familiar things
3. Therefore: maximize iterations (novel attempts) not hours of familiar repetition
4. This requires comfort with frequent small failures â€” each failure is a data point from a new iteration
5. Nassim Taleb's trading strategy exemplifies this: small daily losses (iterations against the market) with rare massive wins when tail events occur
