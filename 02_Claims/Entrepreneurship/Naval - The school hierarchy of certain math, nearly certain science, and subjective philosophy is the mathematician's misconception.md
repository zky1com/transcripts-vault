# Naval - The school hierarchy of certain math, nearly certain science, and subjective philosophy is the mathematician's misconception

## Position

A common academic hierarchy arranges knowledge fields by certainty: mathematics is fully certain (proofs are definitive), science is highly confident (experiments can be wrong but converge), and philosophy is mostly opinion (no experiments to constrain it). This hierarchy is wrong. Mathematics is not certain — it is a creative act performed by fallible humans, subject to errors in axioms and proofs, and constrained by incompleteness. Science is not "almost certain" — it produces provisional best explanations that can always be refuted. Philosophy is not mere opinion — it includes logical and conceptual analysis that can be done well or poorly. The hierarchy mistakes the subject matter of mathematics (necessary truth, which is exact) for our knowledge of it (provisional, fallible, creative). Deutsch calls this "the mathematician's misconception" — the intuitive feeling that a proved theorem is certainly true, when in fact certainty is a property of the subject matter, not of our access to it.

## Evidence Cited

- "people have this idea that mathematics is this pristine area of knowledge where what has proved to be true is certainly true — then you have science which doesn't give you certain truth but you can be highly confident — then of course there's philosophy which is a mere matter of opinion"
- "this is the hierarchy that some people inherit from school: mathematics is certain, science is almost certain, and the rest of it is more or less a matter of opinion"
- "this is what Deutsch calls the mathematician's misconception — it's a confusion between the subject matter and our knowledge of the subject matter"

## Logic Chain

1. School hierarchy: math (certain) > science (confident) > philosophy (opinion)
2. Why math seems certain: proofs derive conclusions necessarily from axioms → feels unchallengeable
3. But: axioms may be wrong; proofs contain human reasoning → errors possible; Gödel limits what is provable
4. Why science seems "almost certain": experiments constrain theories → converges on better theories
5. But: convergence is toward better approximations, not truth; theories always provisional
6. Why philosophy seems like opinion: no experiments → but logical and conceptual analysis can be done well or poorly
7. The hierarchy confuses the nature of the subject matter with the epistemic status of our knowledge
8. Correct view: all three fields produce provisional knowledge; none produces certainty; all require creativity
