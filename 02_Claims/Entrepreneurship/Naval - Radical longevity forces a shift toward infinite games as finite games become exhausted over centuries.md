# Naval - Radical longevity forces a shift toward infinite games as finite games become exhausted over centuries

## Position

Using James Carse's distinction between finite and infinite games, Naval argues that radical longevity structurally requires infinite games — games played not to be won but to keep playing. Finite games have a terminal condition: you win, lose, or complete them. Career, parenting, and even spirituality as conventionally practiced are finite games. Once a finite game is seen through — understood well enough that mystery and challenge are exhausted — it can no longer hold interest. The only games that survive indefinitely are infinite ones: games that reorganize and generate new challenges as you play. For a being with a centuries-long lifespan, the shift is not optional — finite games will be exhausted, and the psychology of long-lived beings must reorganize around pursuit that has no end state. Naval regards this as a genuine intellectual and civilizational challenge, not a trivial problem to be dismissed.

## Evidence Cited

- "the nature of the game that you're playing will change towards much more infinite games and finite games to use James car's terminology"
- "almost any game becomes boring so the challenge for any creature sufficiently long lived is what game to play that I haven't yet seen through and that will last me till the end of days"

## Logic Chain

1. Finite games: played to win or complete — have a terminal condition
2. Infinite games (Carse): played to continue playing — no terminal condition, self-renewing
3. Radical longevity gives enough time to exhaust all finite games
4. Therefore: very long-lived beings are forced toward infinite games by exhaustion of finite ones
5. This is not a preference — it is a structural consequence of the timescale
