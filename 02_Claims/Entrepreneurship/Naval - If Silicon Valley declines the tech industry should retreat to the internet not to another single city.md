# Naval - If Silicon Valley declines the tech industry should retreat to the internet not to another single city

## Position

The Bay Area's politics and policy may eventually make it hostile enough to the tech industry that the clustering begins to dissolve. If that happens, the natural successor is not another single city (Miami, Austin, Seattle) but the internet itself — a geographically distributed network where tech workers and founders are co-present digitally rather than physically. Tech is uniquely positioned for this: it built the remote-work infrastructure, its workers are highly mobile, and its products can be created by small geographically dispersed teams. A "tech city on the internet" is not yet fully realized (the social and serendipitous collision benefits of physical proximity are hard to replicate), but Naval sees it as the long-run equilibrium — a return to first principles: the tech industry should be where its workers genuinely want to be, and for increasingly many of them, "anywhere with good internet" is the honest answer.

## Evidence Cited

- "if it were to disintegrate from here, my hope is that it would retreat to the internet and not to any single geographic location"
- "the technology isn't quite there yet but we're getting closer every day"
- "San Francisco is busy playing 'pluck the golden goose's feathers as far as we can go'"

## Logic Chain

1. SF Bay Area is politically hostile to tech (taxes, regulation, crime, quality of life)
2. If this continues: tech talent and companies will leave
3. Historical pattern: they would move to the best available uncaptured city
4. But: tech has evolved → remote work is now much more viable; small teams; distributed collaboration tools built by tech itself
5. The "best available uncaptured city" may now be "no city" — the internet
6. Tech is uniquely equipped to decouple from physical geography because it built the tools for doing so
7. Therefore: if SF declines, the natural successor is distributed/internet-native rather than another hub city
