# Naval - Principles should be kept high-level and deliberately incomplete — precision makes them less applicable not more

## Position

Naval deliberately keeps his principles vague and high-level, and argues this is not a failure but a feature. Over-precise principles are less useful because they become tied to the specific context in which they were articulated. A highly precise principle says: "In situation X with constraint Y, do Z." That rule only applies to situations resembling X. A high-level principle — "specific knowledge is something you love so much that you'd do it for free" — applies across a vastly wider set of situations because it is not locked to a specific instantiation. The added benefits are memorability (simpler formulations stick) and conversational utility (they invite interpretation rather than closing it down). The cost is that they require the reader to do the work of contextual application — which is precisely the work that builds judgment. Principles that do the application work for you train dependency, not wisdom.

## Evidence Cited

- "I keep my principles high level and incomplete partially because it just sounds better and it's easier to remember, but also just because it's more applicable."

## Logic Chain

1. Highly precise principles are tightly coupled to the context of their derivation
2. When context changes, precise principles fail to transfer
3. High-level principles abstract away context → applicable across more situations
4. They are also more memorable → more frequently retrieved and applied
5. Cost: they require the reader to interpret and apply — but this is the same work that builds judgment
6. Therefore: deliberate incompleteness in principles is a feature, not a failure
