# Naval - The only true test of intelligence is whether you get what you want out of life — including knowing what to want

## Position

Abstract intelligence — IQ, academic credentials, verbal reasoning — is a poor proxy for what actually matters. The only true test is whether you get what you want out of life. This claim has two distinct parts, and the second is harder than the first: (1) Were you able to hack reality and execute well enough to obtain your goals? (2) Were you smart enough to figure out what was worth wanting in the first place? The second part is more important because the world is full of booby prizes — things that look worth having but carry large hidden costs, or that are simply outside your reach. "Not wanting something is as good as having it" — if you can eliminate a desire from the outset that's even better than satisfying it. Many highly credentialed, intellectually impressive people fail this test because they spend their intelligence chasing things that don't make them happy. The single player game framing enables this: you design the victory condition, not just the moves.

## Logic Chain

1. Intelligence in the abstract (IQ, credentials, reasoning tests) is not self-evidently valuable
2. The purpose of intelligence is to navigate reality toward better outcomes
3. The ultimate test of navigation quality is: did you get what you wanted from life?
4. But this has a deeper component: "what you wanted" must itself be intelligently chosen
5. Many intelligent people achieve the wrong goals — they won the game they were playing but lost the game that mattered
6. Therefore true intelligence = (ability to achieve goals) × (quality of goals chosen)
7. This is the single player game in practice: you design the victory condition and then you try to meet it
