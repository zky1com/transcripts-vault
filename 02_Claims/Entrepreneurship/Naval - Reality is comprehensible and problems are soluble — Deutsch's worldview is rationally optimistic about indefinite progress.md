# Naval - Reality is comprehensible and problems are soluble — Deutsch's worldview is rationally optimistic about indefinite progress

## Position

The core of Deutsch's worldview, as Naval reads it, is two axiomatic claims: reality is comprehensible, and problems are soluble. Together these ground a deep rational optimism. If reality is comprehensible, then in principle there is no phenomenon that lies permanently beyond human understanding. If problems are soluble, then every current evil, difficulty, or lack is in principle fixable given enough knowledge. This is not naive optimism — it is the epistemological position that knowledge creation is the fundamental human activity, that it can always continue, and that its continuation always improves things. The "beginning of infinity" metaphor captures the asymmetry: we are at the very start of an infinite process of knowledge creation and improvement, with no fixed endpoint.

## Evidence Cited

- "Deutsche's world view is that reality is comprehensible, problems are soluble — it's a deeply rationally optimistic worldview"
- "it believes in good explanations, good scientific explanations and progress — progress is inevitable as long as we have these good explanations"
- "all sins and evil are due to a lack of knowledge — one can be optimistic about constant progress"
- "that's what the title the beginning of infinity refers to — that we're at the beginning of an infinite series of progress"

## Logic Chain

1. Reality is comprehensible: nothing is inherently beyond human understanding
2. Problems are soluble: every difficulty can in principle be resolved through sufficient knowledge
3. All evil = lack of knowledge → evil is not inherent in reality, it is a knowledge problem
4. Good explanations drive progress → progress is not random but structured by the quality of our theories
5. Progress is open-ended because knowledge can always improve and every theory can be falsified and improved
6. "Beginning of infinity": we are at the start, not near the end — the frontier of knowledge is vast and permanent
