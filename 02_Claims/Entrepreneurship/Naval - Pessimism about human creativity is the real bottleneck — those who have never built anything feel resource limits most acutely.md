# Naval - Pessimism about human creativity is the real bottleneck — those who have never built anything feel resource limits most acutely

## Position

The ultimate constraint on human progress is not matter or energy but the pessimistic belief that human creativity is bounded. Even if all other physical resources were available, a civilization that believed its problem-solving capacity was running out would stop solving problems. Naval observes an empirical pattern: the people who feel most acutely that resources and possibilities are running out tend to be those who have never built anything from scratch. The act of creation teaches, experientially, that problems thought to have no solutions turn out to be solvable once you engage with them directly. Conversely, people who observe from outside the creative process can more easily believe that the space of possible solutions is exhausted. The appropriate prior — grounded in the historical record of human creativity reliably dissolving apparent limits — is that the space of solutions is effectively infinite.

## Evidence Cited

- "there's a pessimistic assumption here that people make that human creativity is bounded and i think it's the people who themselves have not built things who have not created new things from scratch who seem to feel this the most"

## Logic Chain

1. The binding constraint on progress is not physical matter but the belief that solutions exist
2. If you believe creativity is bounded, you stop investing in creative search
3. People who have built things from scratch experience firsthand that seemingly impossible problems become solvable
4. People who have never built have no such experiential correction to pessimistic priors
5. The historical record: human creativity has consistently solved problems that appeared unsolvable
6. Correct prior: assume creativity is unbounded until proven otherwise — the record supports this
