# Naval - Bayesianism works for updating within known hypothesis spaces but cannot generate new explanations...

## Position

Bayesianism is a valid and powerful tool â€” but only within its actual domain of applicability. The Monty Hall problem demonstrates this domain clearly: you have a finite, enumerable hypothesis space (three doors), you receive new information (a door is opened), and you correctly update your probability assignment (switch doors â€” your odds go from 1/3 to 1/2). This is Bayesianism working exactly as intended: new information within a closed hypothesis space produces correct probability revision. The same logic applies to medicine: which of these drugs is more likely to be effective? Bayesianism handles this well because the hypothesis space is defined. But science regularly confronts problems where the hypothesis space is not defined â€” where the creative act of generating a new theory is the missing element. In these cases, Bayesianism provides no help. The mistake is to conclude from Monty Hall success that "being Bayesian" is a general philosophy of knowledge generation. It is a probability revision tool, not a theory-creation tool.

## Logic Chain

1. Monty Hall: finite known hypothesis space (3 doors), new information (opened door), correct update (switch)
2. This is Bayesianism's legitimate domain: closed probability spaces with enumerable outcomes
3. Medicine: which drug works better â€” hypothesis space defined, Bayesian updating valid
4. Scientific theory generation: hypothesis space not enumerable â€” you cannot assign priors to theories that don't exist yet
5. Generating new theories requires creativity â€” an act that Bayesian calculus cannot perform
6. Judging between existing theories requires experimental refutation or argument â€” again, not Bayesian updating
7. Conclusion: Bayesianism is a valid tool for a specific domain; treating it as a general epistemology is a category error
