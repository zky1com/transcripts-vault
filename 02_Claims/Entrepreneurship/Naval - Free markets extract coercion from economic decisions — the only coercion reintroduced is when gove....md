# Naval - Free markets extract coercion from economic decisions — the only coercion reintroduced is when gove...

## Position

The distinguishing feature of free markets is the absence of forced participation: no one is compelled to buy, sell, or enter any agreement. Every transaction is voluntary, which means every transaction is a mutual affirmation that both parties believe they benefit. This is the feedback mechanism that makes markets truth-revealing â€” non-coercive exchange reveals genuine preferences. Government intervention reintroduces coercion by mandating specific outcomes, replacing voluntary preference revelation with top-down edicts enforced by legal threat.

## Evidence Cited

- "what we've extracted out of that decision-making process is the coercion no one is forced into purchasing a service undertaking an agreement"
- "the only time the force is applied is when the government gets involved the people at the top then say this is the best decision and you will all have to agree with it otherwise there's going to be a man with a badge and a gun turn up at your door"
- "all that we're saying when it comes to free market is that the individual gets to decide without being coerced what might work for them"

## Logic Chain

1. Voluntary exchange: both parties consent, both believe they benefit â€” this is the definition of a free market transaction
2. Consent-based exchange reveals genuine preferences, making markets information systems as well as allocation systems
3. Government mandates replace consent with compulsion â€” the individual is no longer choosing, they are obeying
4. This destroys the preference-revelation property: compliance no longer signals genuine valuation
5. Therefore: the case for free markets is fundamentally about the epistemic value of consent, not just allocative efficiency
