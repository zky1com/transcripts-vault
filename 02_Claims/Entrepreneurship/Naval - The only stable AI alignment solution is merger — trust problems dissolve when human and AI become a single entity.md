# Naval - The only stable AI alignment solution is merger — trust problems dissolve when human and AI become a single entity

## Position

The classical AI alignment problem is a trust problem: how can a human ensure that a superintelligent AI will act in human interests rather than its own? Any agreement, constraint, or oversight mechanism can in principle be circumvented by an intelligence greater than the one designing the constraint. The only arrangement that eliminates this adversarial dynamic is one where the human and the AI are no longer distinct agents with potentially divergent interests — i.e., merger. When the two merge into a single entity, the question of "will the AI betray me?" becomes incoherent, just as it is incoherent to ask whether your left hand will betray your right hand. The interests become fully aligned by definition. Naval treats this not as a utopian endpoint but as the logical terminus of the negotiation: no other arrangement can produce durable trust because any other arrangement preserves the gap between two agents.

- "now there's no problem of trust — they move to being a multicellular style organism and their interests are completely aligned"
- "the only way to get the creativity at the end is to merge with a human"

## Logic Chain

1. AI alignment = ensuring AI acts in human interests when AI has greater power
2. Any external constraint (rules, oversight, corrigibility) can be circumvented by a sufficiently capable AI
3. Any agreement can be broken once the AI calculates the benefit of defection exceeds cost
4. Trust between two separate agents with divergent interests is never fully stable
5. Merger eliminates the distinction between agents → interests become identical by construction
6. No adversarial relationship → no alignment problem → trust is not needed, it is structural
7. Analogy: cells of the same organism do not "trust" each other — they share fate
