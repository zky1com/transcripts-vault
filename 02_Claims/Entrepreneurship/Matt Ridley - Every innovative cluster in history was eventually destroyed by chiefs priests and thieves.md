# Matt Ridley - Every innovative cluster in history was eventually destroyed by chiefs priests and thieves

## Position

Throughout history, whenever a region developed an innovation cluster, it was eventually extinguished by one of three forces: chiefs (authoritarian political rulers who restrict and control), priests (religious fundamentalists who condemn inquiry and impose ideological constraints), or thieves (rent-seekers who extract value without creating it). Ming China was extinguished by authoritarian political restriction. Abbasid Arabia's 11th-century flowering of knowledge was crushed by a religious fundamentalist revival. Innovation survived historically only because there was always somewhere else to go — innovative people could escape hostile regimes and start again. In a fully globalized world, the option to escape is diminished. This makes the innovation threat more serious now than in earlier periods.

## Evidence Cited

- "I've always been interested in the fact that these innovation bushfires eventually are extinguished — usually by some combination of chiefs, priests, and thieves"
- "in Ming China it's a very restrictive authoritarian and interfering political regime that kills the goose laying the golden eggs"
- "in Abbasid Arabia a great flowering of knowledge and innovation is crushed by the religious fundamentalist revival when Islam goes from being very open-minded to a very closed-minded structure"
- "in the old days there was always somewhere else to go — in this global world you could imagine a sufficiently malignant cult taking over the entire world and saying no we don't want learning, innovation and technology"

## Logic Chain

1. Innovation requires freedom: to experiment, fail, try again, and share knowledge
2. Three forces oppose freedom: chiefs (political control), priests (ideological control), thieves (economic extraction)
3. Historical pattern: innovation cluster emerges in period of relative freedom → eventually one of the three forces achieves dominance → cluster collapses
4. Ming China: authoritarian restriction → end of Chinese technological leadership
5. Abbasid Arabia: religious fundamentalist takeover → end of Islamic golden age
6. Historical safety valve: innovators could move from hostile regime to welcoming one
7. In a globalized world, there are fewer escape routes → the threat from chiefs/priests/thieves is now more existential
