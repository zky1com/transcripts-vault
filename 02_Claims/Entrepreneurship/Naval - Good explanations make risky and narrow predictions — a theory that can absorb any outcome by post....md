# Naval - Good explanations make risky and narrow predictions — a theory that can absorb any outcome by post...

## Position

The third property of a good explanation: it should make predictions that are not only testable but *risky* â€” specific, quantitative, and narrow enough that they could clearly fail. Naval cites Einstein's general relativity predicting that starlight would bend around the sun during a solar eclipse. This was a risky prediction: the bending angle was specific, it had never been observed, and it took until the 1919 Eddington expedition to confirm it. The prediction staked out a claim that was genuinely hard to absorb into the theory if it had failed. By contrast, vague predictions that can be adjusted after the fact (like the grass example) are epistemically worthless even if technically falsifiable. Riskyness is the measure of how much epistemic work a prediction does.

## Logic Chain

1. A falsifiable theory can still be epistemically worthless if failed tests can be absorbed by adjusting parameters
2. The grass-cure theory is an example: any failure can be explained by changing dose, timing, or variety
3. A good explanation makes specific numerical predictions that could clearly fail â€” like Einstein's prediction of the exact angle of starlight bending
4. Eddington's 1919 eclipse experiment confirmed Einstein's specific prediction â€” this was genuine epistemic progress because the prediction was specific and risky
5. The "riskiness" of a prediction is proportional to how much it would cost the theory if the prediction failed
