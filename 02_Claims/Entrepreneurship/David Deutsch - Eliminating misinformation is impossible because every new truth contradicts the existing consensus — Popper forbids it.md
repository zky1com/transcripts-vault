# David Deutsch - Eliminating misinformation is impossible because every new truth contradicts the existing consensus — Popper forbids it

## Position

Any policy designed to eliminate "misinformation" by suppressing claims that contradict the current scientific consensus will, by construction, suppress future true knowledge. This is not a contingent risk but a logical necessity: every genuine scientific advance begins as a claim that contradicts what was previously believed to be true. Popper's insight is that we cannot in advance distinguish a false claim from a true-but-not-yet-accepted claim — the two look identical from the perspective of current consensus. Therefore, any epistemic authority empowered to remove "misinformation" will inevitably remove true knowledge at the moment it most needs to be heard. The implication is not that all claims are equally valid, but that the mechanism of suppression is fundamentally incompatible with the process of knowledge growth.

## Evidence Cited

- "every new discovery contradicts existing knowledge — that's what makes it new"
- "the mechanism for finding truth is criticism — not by having an authority declare what's true and censor everything else"
- "the Popperian point is that you can't tell in advance which of the things that contradict the consensus are true and which are false"
- "if you suppress everything that contradicts the consensus, you suppress the next true theory along with all the false ones"

## Logic Chain

1. Scientific progress: new knowledge always contradicts some existing belief — this is what makes it new
2. Misinformation policy: suppresses claims that contradict current consensus
3. Popper's point: before a new theory is accepted, it is indistinguishable in form from a false heterodox claim — both contradict the consensus
4. Therefore: any consensus-enforcement mechanism necessarily suppresses true future knowledge
5. This is not a risk to be managed but a logical entailment of the policy structure
6. The correct mechanism for truth-finding is criticism and open debate — not suppression
7. Authorities empowered to suppress "misinformation" are not epistemically neutral arbiters; they are themselves fallible and subject to error
8. Conclusion: misinformation policies are self-defeating — they cannot preserve truth without also destroying the process that generates it
