# Naval - As lifespans extend the challenge shifts from survival to boredom — humans naturally escalate to longer-term games as they exhaust shorter ones

## Position

Human meaning-making follows a predictable escalation pattern. Early in life, trivially short games suffice — soap bubbles, Legos. Then video games lasting days; social games lasting months; career and relationship games lasting years; parenting lasting decades; and finally spiritual practice that might last a lifetime. Each game is sufficient until it is seen through, at which point a longer-horizon game is needed. In a normally-lengthed life this progression roughly exhausts the available games. But radical longevity — lifespans measured in centuries or millennia — would shatter this structure. You would exhaust all currently available games and require games with genuinely open-ended, infinite structure. The problem is not finding more time but finding games that remain meaningful across timescales that no human has ever navigated. Boredom, not disease, becomes the terminal condition.

## Evidence Cited

- "if we extend lifespan the greatest challenge is going to be boredom because the pattern seems to be that when you're young you're amused by very short-term games...then you have children you play the children game and that might last you several decades and then you play The Meditation and spirituality game"
- "if you have a lifespan measured in the hundreds and thousands of years you're going to have to play really long-term games to hold your interest"

## Logic Chain

1. Life follows an observable pattern: humans exhaust short games and escalate to longer-horizon ones
2. Normal lifespans roughly fit the available game lengths — childhood → career → parenting → spirituality
3. Radical longevity (centuries/millennia) would exhaust all these game categories
4. No currently available human game is designed for timescales of thousands of years
5. Therefore: the primary challenge of radical longevity is not biological but existential — finding games that don't get seen through
