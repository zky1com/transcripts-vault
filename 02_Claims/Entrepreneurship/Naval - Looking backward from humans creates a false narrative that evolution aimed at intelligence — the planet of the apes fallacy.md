# Naval - Looking backward from humans creates a false narrative that evolution aimed at intelligence — the planet of the apes fallacy

## Position

There is a systematic cognitive error in how people interpret evolutionary history. Starting from the endpoint — us — and reading backwards, it looks like evolution was climbing a ladder toward intelligence. The high school textbook images of a hunched ape gradually straightening into a briefcase-carrying human reinforce this illusion. But this is survivorship bias applied to evolutionary history: we notice the path that led to us and assign it direction. Charlie Lineweaver's "planet of the apes hypothesis" names this error precisely: the idea that if humans were removed, apes would naturally fill the intelligence niche. This is equivalent to an elephant observing that its lineage has longer and longer trunks and concluding that evolution is optimizing for trunk length. The direction appears only from the animal's own vantage point. Evolution has no target, no direction, and no destination. Intelligence is not an evolutionary niche waiting to be filled.

- "you're an elephant that is able to think about themselves and they reflect on the length of their trunk...ah evolution has been geared towards making ever longer trunks that's what evolution is all about of course we can see that that's ridiculous"

## Logic Chain

1. Survivorship bias: we observe the evolutionary path that led to us and read it as directional
2. Looking backward from an endpoint always creates an apparent trajectory, regardless of whether any direction was actually present
3. Lineweaver's planet of apes: removing humans → apes fill intelligence niche? No — this assumes intelligence is a niche that gets filled, not a historical accident
4. The elephant analogy shows the fallacy: any sufficiently self-aware creature would see its own traits as evolution's goal
5. Therefore: evolution has no direction; intelligence was not targeted; it is a one-time accident on one branch
