# Naval - The ancient project of understanding all reality is not abandoned but revived by Deutsch — modern specialization created a false impression that unified understanding is impossible

## Position

Ancient philosophers believed they could understand everything about the world — a unified theory of reality. As science advanced and specialization deepened, this goal came to seem naive: too much is known across too many fields for any one person to grasp it all. Deutsch's work rehabilitates the ancient aspiration without denying the reality of specialization. His argument is that the right level of analysis is not the detailed facts of each field but the deep structural principles that unify them — epistemology, evolution, computation, and quantum theory. At this level, a unified understanding is possible: not knowing all the facts, but understanding the principles by which knowledge is created, tested, and improved in any domain. This is what drew Naval to Deutsch: the possibility that a single person can again aspire to understand reality as a whole, at the appropriate level of abstraction.

## Logic Chain

1. Ancient aspiration: unified understanding of all reality — achievable because knowledge was limited
2. Modern science: specialization made this seem impossible — too many fields, too much content
3. Deutsch's claim: the impossible is only the detailed content; the structural principles are still unifiable
4. The four strands (epistemology, evolution, computation, quantum theory) unify at the level of deep principles
5. A person can master these principles without mastering all the facts of every field
6. Therefore: the ancient philosophical project is revivable at the right level of abstraction
