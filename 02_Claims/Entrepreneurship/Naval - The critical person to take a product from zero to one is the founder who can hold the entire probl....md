# Naval - The critical person to take a product from zero to one is the founder who can hold the entire probl...

## Position

Getting a product from zero to one requires a single person â€” usually the founder â€” who can hold the entire system in their head: every component, why it's there, and what happens if it's removed. This is not about designing every part or knowing every implementation detail. It's about understanding the trade-offs and interdependencies. This person alone can make the necessary judgment calls about what to eliminate without breaking the system. Without this holistic mental model, complex products accumulate irrelevant requirements that nobody can trace back to their original purpose â€” like fiberglass mats glued to batteries for obsolete reasons.

## Evidence Cited

- "The most critical person to take a great product from zero to one is the single person, usually the founder, who can hold the entire problem in their head and make the trade-offs and understand why each component is where it is"
- "they do need to be able to understand why is this piece here? And if part A gets removed, then what happens to parts B, C, D, E and their requirements and considerations"
- "It's that holistic view of the whole product"

## Logic Chain

1. Products become complex over time as each team adds components for their own reasons
2. Without a single person who understands the whole, each component is justified locally but the system-level trade-offs are invisible
3. The holistic founder can ask "why is this here?" and trace the requirement chain
4. This is the prerequisite for requirement elimination and complexity reduction
5. Therefore: scaling the product (adding specialists, teams) at the expense of the holistic view produces irreversible complexity accumulation
