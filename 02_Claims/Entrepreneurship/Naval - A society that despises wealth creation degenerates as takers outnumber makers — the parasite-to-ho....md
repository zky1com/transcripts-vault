# Naval - A society that despises wealth creation degenerates as takers outnumber makers — the parasite-to-ho...

## Position

Naval uses the biological parasite analogy: any organism can sustain a small number of parasites (some are even symbiotic), but when the parasitic load becomes too large, the host dies. Applied to economics: a society can accommodate some redistribution and some public goods, but when the ratio of net takers to net producers becomes too large, productivity collapses. Groups and ideologies that ideologically despise wealth creation accelerate this collapse by undermining the incentive structure that produces the goods they consume. Venezuela is cited as the contemporary example.

## Evidence Cited

- "if you get too many takers and not enough makers society falls apart... people are literally starving in the streets and losing kilograms of body weight every year just from sheer starvation"
- "imagine an organism that has too many parasites... if you just were filled with parasites... you would die. So any organism can only withstand a small number of parasites"
- "groups that despise it will essentially bring the entire group down to that level"
- "that society will plunge into ruin and darkness"

## Logic Chain

1. Wealth is produced by people who create more value than they consume (makers)
2. Redistribution transfers from makers to takers â€” which is sustainable up to a threshold
3. When ideology attacks the legitimacy of wealth creation, makers reduce production or exit
4. Below a critical maker-to-taker ratio, the surplus that sustains takers evaporates
5. Therefore: the tolerance for anti-market ideology is a function of how large the productive surplus is â€” beyond a threshold, ideological hostility to wealth creation is self-defeating even for those who benefit from redistribution
