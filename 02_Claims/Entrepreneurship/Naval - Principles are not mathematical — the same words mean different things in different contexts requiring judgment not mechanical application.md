# Naval - Principles are not mathematical — the same words mean different things in different contexts requiring judgment not mechanical application

## Position

Principles and aphorisms look like rules but they are not. Mathematics has precise definitions: "2 + 2 = 4" means exactly one thing in all contexts. Principles use natural language terms — "wealth," "rich," "love," "happiness" — that are fundamentally overloaded: the same word means different things in different contexts to different people. This means you cannot form a playbook from principles and execute it like a computer program. The same principle applied mechanically in different contexts will produce the wrong answer. What is required is judgment: the ability to read the specific context, identify which meaning of the overloaded term is relevant, and apply the principle in its contextually appropriate form. This is why principles require extensive field experience to use well — without the experience, you cannot identify which context you are in and therefore which application of the principle is correct.

## Evidence Cited

- "a lot of this kind of general principles and advice is not mathematics. Sometimes you're using the word rich to mean one thing. Other times use it to mean another thing. Same with the word wealth. Same with the word love or happiness. These are overloaded terms. So this is not mathematics. These are not precise definitions. You can't form a playbook out of them that you can just follow like a computer."
- "Instead, you have to understand what context to apply them in."

## Logic Chain

1. Mathematics: precise definitions, universal applicability, mechanical execution possible
2. Natural language principles: overloaded terms whose meaning varies with context
3. "Rich" means financial wealth in one context, freedom in another, meaningful relationships in another
4. Mechanical application of the same rule across all contexts → wrong answers in most contexts
5. Correct use requires context identification → appropriate interpretation → context-specific application
6. This is exactly what judgment is: the ability to read context and apply principles appropriately
