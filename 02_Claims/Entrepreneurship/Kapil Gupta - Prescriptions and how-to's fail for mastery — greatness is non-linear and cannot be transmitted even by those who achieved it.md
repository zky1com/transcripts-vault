# Kapil Gupta - Prescriptions and how-to's fail for mastery — greatness is non-linear and cannot be transmitted even by those who achieved it

## Position

How-to instructions (prescriptions) work for mechanical tasks — learning to drive, assembling furniture — but fail categorically for anything that involves mastery, peak performance, or inner transformation. This applies equally to business, sports, and inner freedom. The mechanism of failure is the same: the prescription becomes the new goal, displacing the original destination. The person now works to satisfy the prescription rather than to achieve the underlying aim. More fundamentally, greatness is non-linear, messy, and unpredictable in process — even the person who achieved it cannot retrace their steps and reproduce it, let alone transmit it to someone else. Roger Federer cannot explain how he hits the ball; Warren Buffett cannot transmit the details of his investment thinking. The greatest things a person does are the things they themselves don't know how they did. This is not a deficiency — it is the nature of the territory. The answer is not a better prescription but genuine, obsessive engagement with the problem itself.

## Logic Chain

1. Prescriptions work for mechanical tasks because the process is defined and repeatable
2. For mastery domains — business, sports, inner freedom — the process is non-linear, unpredictable, and highly individual
3. The prescription becomes the new object of attention, displacing the original goal
4. Even those who achieved greatness cannot reproduce it by retracing their steps — the process was unique and largely unconscious
5. The details are not merely hard to transmit — they are unknowable even to the person who did them
6. Therefore copying prescriptions of great achievers reliably produces failure, not replication
7. The correct approach is obsessive direct engagement with the problem — not seeking a how-to but developing one's own path through the jungle
