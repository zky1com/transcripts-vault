# David Sacks - Rent control combined with restrictive zoning removes builder incentives and worsens affordability

## Position
Freeberg argues that governments pursuing housing affordability through rent control and restrictive zoning achieve the opposite of their intent. Capping rent upside kills equity returns for building owners, eliminating investment incentives, while regulation-driven cost increases make new construction financially unviable. The compounding result is reduced housing supply and higher prices â€” the exact opposite of the policy's goal.

## Evidence Cited
- Transcript: "LA city council held a vote 12 to 2... the landlord cannot increase the rent on an annual basis by more than 90% of CPI with a floor of 1% and a cap of 4%."
- Transcript: "that creates a disincentive for capital for investors to buy new buildings or put money into upgrading buildings or put money into building new buildings."
- Transcript: "the government has passed law after law, regulation after regulation, statute after statute that makes it more expensive, take more time, and more difficult to build housing."
- Transcript: "Every time the government gets involved in a market, it distorts the market. It limits the flow of liquidity and it limits the market finding lower prices."
- Transcript: "same thing happened with Prop 13... which creates a huge disincentive for people to sell their homes and reduces liquidity in the market."

## Logic Chain
1. Rent control limits landlord revenue upside, reducing equity return on residential buildings.
2. Lower returns eliminate financial incentive for buying, upgrading, or building rental units.
3. Simultaneously, regulations increase construction costs and timelines, discouraging new supply.
4. Excess mortgage liquidity via Fannie/Freddie supports buyer demand without adding supply.
5. The combined demand support plus supply suppression drives prices up despite affordability intent.
