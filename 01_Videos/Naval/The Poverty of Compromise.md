## Summary

Naval argues that compromise is often intellectually sterile when it mixes two rival ideas into a third option that neither side actually believes. Drawing on Popper's view of democracy and science, he frames progress as choosing systems that let bad ideas be removed, not systems that pretend midpoint solutions are inherently wise.

## Key Insights

- Democracy is better understood as a mechanism for removing rulers without violence than as a method for selecting perfect rulers.
- Science advances by discarding bad theories, not by averaging competing explanations together.
- Compromise between two ideas often yields a policy no one thought was correct in the first place.
- When a compromise fails, little is learned because each side simply returns to its original view.
- Error correction requires clear, testable alternatives more than polite blending.

## Key Quotes

> Democracy is the system which allows you to remove policies and rulers most efficiently without violence.

> Science is an error-correcting mechanism.

> This is the poverty of compromise.
