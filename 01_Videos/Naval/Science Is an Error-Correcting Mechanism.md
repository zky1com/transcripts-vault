## Summary

Naval argues that science is not a machine for projecting past patterns into the future. It is an explanatory and error-correcting process: scientists invent theories creatively, test them against reality, and improve knowledge by discovering where those theories fail.

## Key Insights

- Induction cannot generate robust explanations because repeated past observations can still break suddenly.
- The black swan problem shows why confidence based only on repetition is intellectually fragile.
- Science explains events by modeling underlying causes, not by merely extending visible trends.
- Observations matter, but only in the context of theories that tell us what they mean.
- Error correction is the core of scientific progress because refutation removes bad explanations and sharpens better ones.

## Key Quotes

> Science is an explanatory framework.

> It's an error-correcting mechanism.

> Science is not about cataloging a history of events that have occurred in the past and presuming they're going to occur again in the future.
