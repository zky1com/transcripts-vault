## Summary

Naval explains that falsifiability alone is too weak a standard for serious knowledge. A strong explanation is hard to vary without breaking its predictive power, because its details are constrained by mechanism rather than adjusted after the fact to fit any outcome.

## Key Insights

- A theory can be testable and still useless if it lacks a credible mechanism.
- Explanations that can absorb every counterexample through parameter tweaks are not making progress.
- Precision matters only when there is a reason for each precise element in the theory.
- Hard-to-vary explanations generate narrow and risky predictions that could genuinely fail.
- Scientific progress depends on ruling out flexible stories in favor of explanations that would break if altered.

## Key Quotes

> Falsifiable theories are actually a dime a dozen.

> The second piece of a good explanation is hard to vary.

> The pieces of that theory should be hard to vary without essentially destroying that theory.

## Claims

- [[Naval - Falsifiability is necessary but not sufficient for a good explanation — any theory can be made to fit the data by adjusting parameters after the fact]]
- [[Naval - A good explanation must provide a mechanistic reason for each of its parameters — a theory without a mechanism cannot be evaluated even if it is technically testable]]
- [[Naval - Good explanations are hard to vary — changing any component of the explanation destroys its predictive power rather than preserving it]]
- [[Naval - Good explanations make risky and narrow predictions — a theory that can absorb any outcome by post-hoc parameter adjustment is not a good explanation]]
