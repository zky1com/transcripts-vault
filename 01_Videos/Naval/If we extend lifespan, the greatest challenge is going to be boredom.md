## Summary

Naval suggests that radical longevity would not just extend our current lives mechanically; it would force a redesign of the kinds of games that make life meaningful. If people live for centuries, the main challenge may become boredom, loneliness, and the need for ever more open-ended pursuits rather than mere survival.

## Key Insights

- Human interests seem to evolve toward longer-horizon games as life progresses.
- A vastly longer lifespan would require goals that remain meaningful over much larger time scales.
- Longevity is still desirable, but it changes the psychological problem set rather than eliminating it.
- Finite games eventually become exhausted, so very long lives may push people toward more infinite games.
- Naval treats lifespan extension as a choice-expanding good while still taking its existential costs seriously.

## Key Quotes

> If we extend lifespan, the greatest challenge is going to be boredom.

> You're going to have to play really long-term games to hold your interest.

> I'll take the choice of health and lifespan over old age and death.
