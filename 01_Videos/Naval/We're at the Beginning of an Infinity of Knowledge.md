## Summary

Naval's point is that short-term incentives often produce shallow behavior, while long-term orientation changes both strategy and character. When you extend the time horizon of a game, you can invest in trust, reputation, learning, and compounding advantages that do not exist in one-shot interactions.

## Key Insights

- Many low-quality behaviors are rational only inside short-term games with no future consequences.
- A longer time horizon makes honesty, patience, and relationship-building more profitable.
- Reputation compounds when the same people expect to deal with each other repeatedly.
- Long-term thinking converts tactical optimization into strategic leverage.
- Better games are often created not by changing your effort but by changing the time horizon.

## Key Quotes

> Turn short-term games into long-term games.

> Play long-term games with long-term people.

> Once the game gets longer, trust starts to matter.
