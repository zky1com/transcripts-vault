## Summary

Naval argues that once a truth-seeking domain becomes too social, its incentives shift away from reality and toward harmony, funding, status, and policy usefulness. Consensus helps groups stay together, but it also tends to corrupt the search for what is actually true.

## Key Insights

- Social groups optimize for cohesion, which naturally pushes them toward compromise rather than truth.
- Natural science worked unusually well when individuals could seek truth somewhat insulated from group incentives.
- Social sciences are vulnerable because they are shaped both by funding pressures and by their effects on policy.
- Groupthink increases social smoothness while often increasing distance from reality.
- Individual truth seekers have historically produced breakthroughs despite social resistance rather than because of group approval.

## Key Quotes

> Making something social destroys the truth of it.

> The more groupthink you see involved, the further from the truth you actually are.

> Consensus is all about compromise, not about truth seeking.
