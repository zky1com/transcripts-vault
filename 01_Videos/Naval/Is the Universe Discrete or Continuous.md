## Summary

Naval frames one of physics' deepest open questions as a conflict between two incompatible intuitions: quantum theory points toward discreteness, while general relativity treats space and time as continuously divisible. The unresolved tension matters because whichever side is fundamental will shape any future unification of physics.

## Key Insights

- Planck units are not automatically the smallest meaningful units of reality.
- Quantum theory encourages a worldview built from indivisible units and quanta.
- General relativity assumes a smooth spacetime that can be divided without obvious limit.
- The clash between discreteness and continuity is not a side issue but part of the central physics problem.
- Any eventual synthesis may require subordinating one framework to the other.

## Key Quotes

> Is the fundamental nature of reality that things can be infinitely divisible, or is it that we must stop somewhere?

> In general relativity, space is infinitely divisible.

> Quantum theory seems to suggest that things are discrete.

## Claims

- [[Naval - Quantum mechanics and general relativity have an irreconcilable foundational contradiction — QM implies discreteness while GR implies infinite divisibility of spacetime]]
- [[Naval - The QM-GR unification problem is fundamentally about whether reality is discrete or continuous — the answer will determine which theory is subservient to the other]]
