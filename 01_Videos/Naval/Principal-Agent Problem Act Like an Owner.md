## Summary

Naval treats the principal-agent problem as one of the most universal failures in organizations. Owners care about the thing itself; agents often optimize for appearances, local incentives, or personal upside, so the key managerial challenge is aligning people closely enough that they start thinking like principals.

## Key Insights

- Misalignment is everywhere because agents optimize according to their own incentives, not automatically the owner's.
- Incentive design is difficult because people are motivated by status, meaning, politics, and self-image, not money alone.
- Founders should overinvest in aligning top lieutenants because high-trust principal-like agents are rare and valuable.
- Small firms and direct accountability often outperform large firms where the real decision-maker is insulated from consequences.
- Acting like an owner while still an employee is one of the fastest ways to earn trust, leverage, and promotion.

## Key Quotes

> If you want it done, then go. If not, then send.

> If you can be working on incentives, don't work on anything else.

> If you're an agent, the best way to operate is to think like a principal.
