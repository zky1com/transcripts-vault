## Summary

Part 2 pushes deeper into Deutsch''s philosophy of science. Naval and Deutsch argue that knowledge starts from problems, not certainty; that explanations matter more than rote prediction; and that science, politics, and philosophy all fail when they pretend to have final answers instead of preserving criticism and error correction.

## Key Insights

- There is no final theory after which humanity can stop thinking, because solving problems creates new ones.
- Science is about explanatory progress, not just collecting successful predictions or measurements.
- Truly decisive experiments between rival explanations are rarer than most people assume.
- Ideas like final certainty, unquestionable authority, or censorship of "misinformation" conflict with the logic of knowledge growth.
- Criticizability is broader and more fundamental than testability, especially outside narrow empirical science.

## Key Quotes

> It all begins with problems.

> There is no royal road to solving them.

> Knowledge is impossible a priori; it has to be creatively conjectured and discovered.
