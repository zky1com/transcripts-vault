## Summary

Naval uses an analogy to particle physics to argue that mathematics, despite dealing in necessary truth, is still practiced by fallible humans. The subject matter may be exact, but our access to it remains provisional, creative, and vulnerable to error.

## Key Insights

- Confusing the topic of mathematics with our current knowledge of it creates a false impression of certainty.
- Mathematics can contain human error for the same reason any other intellectual activity can: thinkers are physical beings who make mistakes.
- The history of physics shows that what looks fundamental today may later turn out to have deeper structure underneath.
- Mathematical progress is creative because new explanations and frameworks must still be invented.
- Even formal domains should be treated as corrigible rather than final.

## Key Quotes

> The subject matter of mathematics is necessary truth.

> A mathematician is just as fallible as anyone else.

> Even mathematics is capable of error because mathematics is a creative act.
