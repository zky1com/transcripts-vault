## Summary

Naval says that great relationships compound like capital: the trust built over years removes friction, uncertainty, and transaction costs. In startups and partnerships, that accumulated trust can matter more than people realize because it keeps teams aligned when stakes are high and decisions are hard.

## Key Insights

- Long-term trust makes collaboration faster because less energy is spent on protection and verification.
- In startups, founder breakdown is an underappreciated cause of failure.
- The biggest benefits of compounding show up late, which is why people underestimate deep relationships early.
- A few strong, durable relationships are often more valuable than a wide but shallow network.
- Naval links relational compounding with ambition: if effort compounds, it is rational to think bigger.

## Key Quotes

> Relationships are a good example of compound interest.

> Most of the benefits of compounding come at the end.

> A few deep compounding relationships are better than a large amount of non-compounding ones.
