## Summary

Naval argues that mathematics should not be treated as a finished cathedral of certainty standing above every other field. Drawing on Taleb, Godel, and Chaitin, he presents mathematics as another domain where truth exists but human access to it remains incomplete, creative, and perpetually revisable.

## Key Insights

- Black swan logic shows why no amount of prior confirmation delivers absolute certainty.
- Godel's incompleteness theorem limits what any formal system can prove from within itself.
- Chaitin's interpretation turns that limitation into an opening for creativity rather than despair.
- The common school hierarchy of certain math, nearly certain science, and subjective philosophy is misleading.
- The real mistake is confusing the certainty of mathematical subject matter with the certainty of our present proofs and interpretations.

## Key Quotes

> There is no settled mathematics.

> At some deep level mathematics is still an art.

> It's a confusion between the subject matter and our knowledge of the subject matter.
