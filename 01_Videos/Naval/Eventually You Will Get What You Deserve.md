## Summary

Naval says wealth usually arrives on a long and uneven timetable. The practical task is to combine specific knowledge, leverage, judgment, and accountability, then stay in the game long enough for compounding to work instead of quitting early because you were counting too closely.

## Key Insights

- Long-term success often looks slow because the payoff comes after years of invisible accumulation.
- Keeping score too closely can destroy the patience required for compounding.
- In entrepreneurship, many failures are acceptable because a single major success can dominate the final outcome.
- Complex decision formulas often create false precision; the best guide is usually the few variables that matter most.
- If you are doing work you are good at and the market values, many other advantages tend to fall into place over time.

## Key Quotes

> You really only have to be right once.

> On a long enough time scale you do get paid.

> If you're counting, you will run out of patience before it actually arrives.
