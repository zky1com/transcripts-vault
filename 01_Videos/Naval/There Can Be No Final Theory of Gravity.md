## Summary

Naval argues that gravity illustrates the Popperian view of science: theories improve by surviving competition, not by accumulating certainty. General relativity is our best current explanation, but that does not make it final, and Bayesian confidence built on repeated past success can still collapse the moment a better rival appears.

## Key Insights

- Crucial experiments matter most when two real theories are competing and one can be ruled out.
- Repeated confirmation does not convert a theory into final truth.
- Newtonian gravity contained useful truth while still being false in important ways.
- General relativity is better than Newton's theory without being the end of inquiry.
- Human intuition defaults toward pattern-based Bayesian reasoning, but explanatory reasoning is a higher and harder cognitive move.

## Key Quotes

> There can be no final theory of gravity.

> All we have is better and better approximations to reality.

> The day before it was shown to be false was the day when you were most confident in it.
