## Summary

Naval argues that resource pessimism usually smuggles in a hidden assumption that human knowledge has stopped growing. Things become resources only when people discover how to transform and use them, so scarcity is not just about what exists in nature but about what explanations and techniques civilization can create.

## Key Insights

- Waste critiques often pretend businesses should have perfect foresight, which is an impossible epistemic standard.
- Calling something a resource always depends on knowledge about how to use it, transport it, or transform it.
- A caveman inhabited the same planet but had access to far fewer resources because he had far less knowledge.
- Finite-resource worldviews typically ignore the open-ended growth of knowledge.
- Innovation repeatedly turns previously useless matter into valuable assets.

## Key Quotes

> To a caveman very few things are resources.

> A resource is just something that through knowledge you can convert from one thing to another.

> This finite resource model of the world implicitly assumes finite knowledge.
