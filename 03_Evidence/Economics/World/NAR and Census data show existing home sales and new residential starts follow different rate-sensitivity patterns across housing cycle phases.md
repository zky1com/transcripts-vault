# NAR and Census data show existing home sales and new residential starts follow different rate-sensitivity patterns across housing cycle phases

## Core Evidence
NAR data shows 2024 existing home sales at 4.06 million — lowest since 1995 — while Census Bureau new residential construction data showed new starts remaining more resilient in absolute terms (approximately 1.4 million starts in 2024). This divergence illustrates that existing-home turnover and new construction respond differently to the same rate environment: existing owners are locked in by the "rate lock-in" effect, while builders can price new-home incentives to keep activity moving.

## External Source

## Verification Notes
- Both data sources confirmed on 2026-02-26; the divergence between existing-home suppression and comparatively resilient new starts is documented in public data.

## Limits
- Both metrics are rate-sensitive over the full cycle; the divergence is strongest in the initial years of rate elevation when the lock-in effect is sharpest and before affordability fully normalizes.
