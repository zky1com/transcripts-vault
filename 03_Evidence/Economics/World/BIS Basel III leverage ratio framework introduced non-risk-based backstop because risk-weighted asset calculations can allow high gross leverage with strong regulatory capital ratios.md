# BIS Basel III leverage ratio framework introduced non-risk-based backstop because risk-weighted asset calculations can allow high gross leverage with strong regulatory capital ratios

## Core Evidence
The Bank for International Settlements (BIS) Basel III framework explicitly acknowledges that risk-weighted asset (RWA) capital ratios can diverge significantly from simple leverage ratios. Basel III therefore introduced a supplementary leverage ratio (SLR) as a "non-risk-based backstop" — precisely because RWA-based capital requirements allow banks with large holdings of zero or low-risk-weighted assets (e.g., government bonds) to report strong regulatory capital ratios while maintaining very high gross balance-sheet leverage. The leverage ratio intentionally treats all assets equally regardless of risk weight.

- BIS FSI Summaries and BCBS leverage ratio framework documents confirmed on 2026-02-26; the non-risk-based backstop rationale for the leverage ratio is explicitly stated in Basel III documentation.

## Limits
- The binding constraint varies by bank type: universal banks are more often constrained by RWA ratios, while custody banks and broker-dealers may be constrained by the SLR due to higher concentrations of low-risk assets.
