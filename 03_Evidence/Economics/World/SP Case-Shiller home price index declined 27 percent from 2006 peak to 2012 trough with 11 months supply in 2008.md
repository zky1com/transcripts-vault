# SP Case-Shiller home price index declined 27 percent from 2006 peak to 2012 trough with 11 months supply in 2008

## Core Evidence
The S&P/Case-Shiller U.S. National Home Price Index peaked in April 2006 and declined approximately 27% in nominal terms before reaching its trough in February 2012. At peak market stress, existing home months supply — the ratio of unsold inventory to monthly sales pace — reached approximately 11 months in July 2008, compared to the 5-6 months considered a balanced market. US housing starts fell from approximately 2.07 million in 2005 (peak) to approximately 478,000 in 2011 — a 77% decline. Major homebuilder stocks including KB Home, Toll Brothers, Lennar, and D.R. Horton declined 60-80% from their 2005-2006 peaks, often before broad institutional recognition of systemic housing risk. This episode provides the primary historical context for the claim's framework: in late-cycle housing downturns, fundamental thesis correctness (rising inventory, deteriorating builder ROE, investor exit pressure) can precede market price discovery by 12-24 months, creating significant timing risk even when the underlying analysis is accurate.

## Limits
- The 27% decline is the national nominal average; regional markets showed much wider variation (Las Vegas, Phoenix, Miami fell 50%+ from peak). The claim's discussion of homebuilder stocks is based on Eisman's personal experience during that cycle and may reference specific names that underperformed or outperformed the broad homebuilder index.
