# Section 301 tariffs impose 7.5 to 25 percent duties on Chinese consumer goods including household and personal products with estimated annual cost of 1300 dollars per US household

## Core Evidence
US Section 301 tariffs on Chinese goods impose rates of 7.5% to 25% across four lists of product categories. List 3 (covering $200B of imports including household goods and personal-care products) carries a 25% rate; List 4A (covering consumer electronics, apparel, and footwear) carries 7.5%. The Peterson Institute for International Economics and Yale Budget Lab estimate these tariffs impose an average cost of approximately $1,300 per US household annually. Consumer staples companies with significant China-based manufacturing or sourcing — including household products, personal care, and food packaging companies — face direct input-cost pressure from these tariffs that is structural rather than cyclical. This cost exposure can erode the pricing-power and margin stability that defines "defensive" sector characteristics, causing underperformance relative to sectors with lower China supply-chain exposure, such as AI-linked semiconductors whose demand is driven by secular technology investment rather than policy-sensitive cost structures.

## Verification Notes
- Section 301 tariff rates confirmed from USTR official tariff action page on 2026-02-26; household cost estimate from Yale Budget Lab distributional analysis of 2025 tariff actions, consistent with Peterson Institute estimates.

## Limits
- The $1,300/household figure represents the aggregate cost of all 2025 tariff actions, not Section 301 alone; the Section 301 contribution is a subset. Individual company tariff exposure depends heavily on sourcing geography and ability to pass through cost increases via price hikes. Some consumer staples companies have partially diversified supply chains away from China since 2018.
