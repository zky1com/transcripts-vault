# Federal Reserve allowed M2 to contract 33 percent from 1929 to 1933 contributing to 9000 bank failures

## Core Evidence
Following the October 1929 stock market crash (the DJIA fell 89% from its 1929 peak to its 1932 trough), the Federal Reserve failed at both stages of effective crisis management. In the preventive stage, the Fed raised its discount rate to 6% in August 1929 partly to cool equity speculation, but took no earlier pre-emptive measures to curb the speculative excess that had built up during 1927-1929. In the crisis containment stage, the Fed allowed the US M2 money supply to contract approximately 33% from 1929 to 1933 — as documented by Milton Friedman and Anna Schwartz in "A Monetary History of the United States, 1867-1960" (1963) — failing to act as a lender of last resort to prevent bank failures from cascading. Approximately 9,000 US banks failed between 1929 and 1933, wiping out deposits of millions of Americans before Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation protection was established (1933). US unemployment reached approximately 25% at the depth of the Depression in 1933. The Federal Reserve's passivity was constrained partly by gold standard orthodoxy and partly by absence of clear institutional authority and political backing for emergency action. This episode is the primary historical example for the claim's two-stage policy framework: failing at prevention allowed speculative excess to build, and failing at rapid emergency liquidity amplified the systemic collapse rather than containing it.

## Limits
- The 33% M2 contraction and its causal attribution to Fed inaction reflects the Friedman-Schwartz "monetarist" interpretation, which while widely accepted is not universally agreed upon — some economists argue the gold standard constraint was the primary binding factor rather than Fed passivity per se. DJIA 89% trough represents the intraday low; the closing low was approximately 86% below the 1929 peak.
